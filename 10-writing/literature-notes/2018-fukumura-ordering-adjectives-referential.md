# Ordering Adjectives in Referential Communication

## Metadata

- Authors: Kumiko Fukumura
- Year: 2018
- Venue: Journal of Memory and Language, 101, 37–50
- DOI / URL: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jml.2018.03.003
- Local PDF path: `10-writing/literatures/Fukumura - 2018 - Ordering adjectives in referential communication.pdf`
- Tags: adjective ordering, discriminatory efficiency, availability, referential communication, color, pattern, priming, dialogue, PASS model

## One-Paragraph Summary

Fukumura contrasts two hypotheses about how speakers determine adjective order in referential communication: the discriminatory efficiency hypothesis (the most discriminating adjective is placed first to facilitate listener identification) and the availability-based ordering hypothesis (more readily available adjectives are placed first to ease production). Using color and pattern adjectives (both context-independent, unlike size), three experiments show that both factors influence ordering: speakers place the more discriminating adjective first (supporting discriminatory efficiency), but they also show a general preference for color before pattern regardless of discriminability (supporting availability). These effects replicate in a dialogue setting (Experiments 2 and 3), where adjective ordering is also primed by the conversational partner's ordering. The paper proposes the PASS (Production and Selection of Specifiers) model to account for the simultaneous influence of discriminability and availability.

## Research Question

- Does discriminatory efficiency (which adjective best distinguishes the target) or production availability determine the order of adjectives in referential communication?

## Method / Data

- Design: 3 experiments; referential communication tasks with visual arrays; color-relevant vs. pattern-relevant contexts
- Participants/Data: Native English-speaking participants; recruited from university pool; Experiment 1 uses naïve speaker-listener pairs; Experiments 2 and 3 use confederate dialogue paradigms
- Measures: Proportion of color-before-pattern vs. pattern-before-color orders; overspecification rates
- Analysis approach: Mixed logistic regression models; within-subject context manipulation

## Key Findings

1. In pattern-relevant contexts (pattern discriminates the target), speakers use more pattern-before-color orders than in color-relevant contexts — supporting discriminatory efficiency (Experiment 1).
2. A general preference for color-before-pattern orders persists across contexts, indicating color adjectives are more available in production — supporting availability-based ordering (Experiments 1–3).
3. In dialogue (Experiments 2–3), adjective ordering is primed by the partner's ordering, demonstrating that pragmatic and structural priming co-determine order even beyond discriminability and availability.

## Core Takeaways For The Current Project

- Directly motivates the DISCRIMINATORY STRENGTH hypothesis used in Schlotterbeck and Wang (2023), which is one of the two hypotheses tested in our experiment.
- The finding that both discriminability and availability contribute is important: our RSA model captures discriminability through the listener's referent identification process, but availability (conceptual salience of color) may be a separate factor not yet in our model.
- The color > pattern availability finding maps onto the broader pattern that color adjectives are overproduced relative to other adjective types — relevant to our experimental stimuli which use color, size, and form adjectives.

## Limitations / Boundary Conditions

- Only color and pattern adjectives are tested; these are both context-independent/absolute, so the results may not generalize to size (context-dependent) adjectives or subjective evaluative adjectives.
- The discriminatory efficiency effect is not large in absolute terms; the availability preference (color before pattern) is stronger and more consistent.
- The PASS model is descriptive rather than computationally specified in terms of RSA utilities.

## Relevance To Current Writing Tasks

- Supports: Including discriminability as a factor in adjective ordering models; motivates the DISCRIMINATORY STRENGTH arm of our model comparison.
- Contrasts with: Pure subjectivity accounts that predict ordering purely from semantic properties without reference to the discriminatory context.
- Open questions for us: Can availability (as operationalized by overspecification rates) be incorporated into the RSA prior over utterances, linking it to the cost function?
