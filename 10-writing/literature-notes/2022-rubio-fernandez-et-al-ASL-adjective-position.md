## One-Paragraph Summary

This paper tests the incremental efficiency hypothesis in American Sign Language (ASL), a language with flexible noun-phrase word order. Three production experiments show that deaf ASL signers tend to place absolute adjectives (color, material) in prenominal position, consistent with the incremental efficiency hypothesis: absolute adjectives can guide visual search before the noun. Scalar adjectives (size) show a more complex pattern — prenominal when expressed as lexical signs, but postnominal when expressed as classifier constructions, where the noun identity must be known first for the size/shape specification to be interpretable. Age of ASL exposure also modulates classifier use. The results suggest that linguistic, pragmatic, and developmental factors jointly constrain referential choice in sign language, supporting efficiency as a cross-modal constraint on language structure.

## Research Question

- Does the incremental efficiency hypothesis extend to American Sign Language, a language with flexible adjective-noun order? Does adjective semantics (absolute vs. scalar) and sign type (lexical vs. classifier) modulate adjective position?

## Method / Data

- Design: Three reference production experiments with deaf ASL signers; standard referential communication task (speaker describes target to listener)
- Participants: Deaf ASL signers; subdivided by age of ASL exposure (early vs. late)
- Measures: Proportion of prenominal vs. postnominal adjective production; classifier vs. lexical sign use
- Analysis approach: Mixed-effects logistic regression; comparison by adjective type, sign type, and age of exposure

## Key Findings

1. Absolute adjectives (color, material) are predominantly produced in prenominal position, consistent with incremental efficiency: they guide visual search before the noun.
2. Scalar adjectives (size) show split behavior: prenominal as lexical signs, postnominal as classifier constructions — because classifiers encode size/shape relative to the referent, requiring noun identity first.
3. Early-exposed signers produce more classifiers than late-exposed signers in some conditions, suggesting developmental factors modulate referential choice.

## Core Takeaways For The Current Project

- Provides cross-modal evidence that absolute vs. scalar adjective distinction interacts with word order efficiency — directly relevant to our model which handles color (absolute) and size (scalar) adjectives.
- The classifier result elegantly illustrates why scalar adjectives are harder to place prenominally: their interpretation is noun-dependent, paralleling the linguistic subjectivity literature on adjective ordering.
- Strengthens the claim that prenominal placement of color adjectives is efficiency-driven, not arbitrary: the effect holds in a language where postnominal placement is also grammatical.

## Limitations / Boundary Conditions

- ASL signers face unique attentional constraints in the visual-world paradigm (must attend to both signer and display), complicating direct comparison with spoken-language studies.
- Production-only; no comprehension or RT data for ASL.
- Age-of-exposure effects are correlational; developmental mechanisms not directly tested.
