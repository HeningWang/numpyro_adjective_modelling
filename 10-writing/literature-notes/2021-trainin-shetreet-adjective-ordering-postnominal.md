# It's a Dotted Blue Big Star: On Adjective Ordering in a Post-Nominal Language

## Metadata

- Authors: Nitzan Trainin, Einat Shetreet
- Year: 2021
- Venue: Language, Cognition and Neuroscience, 36(3), 320–341
- DOI / URL: https://doi.org/10.1080/23273798.2020.1839664
- Local PDF path: `10-writing/literatures/Trainin and Shetreet - 2021 - It's a dotted blue big star on adjective ordering in a post-nominal language.pdf`

## One-Paragraph Summary

Trainin and Shetreet investigate adjective ordering preferences in Hebrew (a postnominal language) and compare them to English (prenominal) using three methodological tasks: free production, naturalness rating, and forced-choice. Their main question is whether Hebrew preferences mirror English preferences in the reverse (as predicted by universal hierarchy accounts). English speakers showed robust ordering preferences (size > color > pattern in the prenominal direction). Hebrew speakers showed significantly weaker preferences overall, and for some adjective combinations the weakly preferred Hebrew orders did not straightforwardly mirror English. The authors argue that additional factors beyond universal semantic hierarchies must be considered for postnominal languages, including structural differences in how adjectives relate to the noun and the processing demands of postnominal modification.

## Research Question

- Do Hebrew (postnominal) speakers show adjective ordering preferences that mirror English preferences in reverse?
- How strong are these preferences, and do they hold for two-adjective and three-adjective strings?

## Method / Data

- Design: 4 experiments: Exp 1 (free production), Exp 2 (naturalness rating), Exp 3 (forced-choice 2-adj and 3-adj), Exp 4 (forced-choice, extended adjective classes); bilingual English-Hebrew comparison
- Participants/Data: Native monolingual Hebrew speakers (N = 27 in Exp 1, larger in others) and native monolingual English speakers; recruited via social media and Prolific
- Measures: Proportion of each adjective order produced; naturalness ratings; forced-choice proportions
- Analysis approach: Descriptive statistics; binomial tests; between-language comparisons

## Key Findings

1. English speakers show robust ordering preferences: size–color–pattern–noun, consistent with prior literature.
2. Hebrew speakers show significantly weaker preferences for all adjective combinations; the preferred order across tasks is noun–color–size (the mirror image in most cases) but with much lower consistency than English.
3. For some adjective combinations (especially size + color + pattern), Hebrew preferences did not straightforwardly mirror English — the observed Hebrew order was not always the expected reverse.
4. The difference in preference strength between English and Hebrew suggests that prenominal modification is associated with stronger/more conventionalized ordering constraints than postnominal modification.

## Core Takeaways For The Current Project

- Relevant for cross-linguistic framing of our paper: if ordering preferences are modulated by word order typology (prenominal vs. postnominal), the efficiency account must explain this asymmetry in preference strength, as Rubio-Fernández et al. (2021) and Waldon & Degen (2021) argue.
- The finding that Hebrew preferences are weaker is consistent with the incremental efficiency hypothesis: in postnominal languages, adjectives come after the noun, reducing the incremental benefit of placing a highly informative adjective first; this reduces the pressure to adopt any particular order.
- Our model focuses on prenominal English, where preferences are strong, so we are in the highest-signal regime — but cross-linguistic extensions should account for the Hebrew pattern.

## Limitations / Boundary Conditions

- Hebrew has specific morpho-phonological features (adjective agreement) that may affect ordering choices; these are not fully controlled.
- The production experiment uses a single-object display (no referential competitors), which may not elicit the same ordering preferences as a referential communication task.
- Sample sizes are relatively small and participants were recruited online, which may introduce noise.
