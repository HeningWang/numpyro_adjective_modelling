## One-Paragraph Summary

Jumelet et al. ask whether large language models (LMs) implicitly learn human-like adjective ordering preferences (AOPs), and whether those preferences can be explained by factors identified in theoretical linguistics (subjectivity, information theory, syntactic class). They build a reusable corpus of adjective pairs and define AOP measures applicable to LMs, then study a series of LMs across intermediate training checkpoints. They find that all models' predicted AOPs are much closer to human preferences than predictions from any single linguistic factor, yet the LM preferences are also not simply a product of learning corpus frequency distributions. The paper suggests LMs may be learning a deeper, more integrated representation of adjective ordering than any single theorized factor, though what exactly this representation encodes remains unclear.

## Research Question

- Do language models learn adjective ordering preferences (AOPs) that match human preferences?
- If so, are these preferences reducible to the theoretical factors proposed in linguistics (subjectivity, information-theoretic measures, syntactic class)?

## Method / Data

- Design: Corpus-based evaluation of LM predictions; comparison to human AOP data and corpus AOP frequency
- Participants/Data: Adjective pair corpus derived from The Pile; LM family is Pythia (12B and intermediate checkpoints); human AOP norms from prior literature
- Measures: AOP-Δ (difference in log-probability between two orderings of an adjective pair)
- Analysis approach: Correlation of LM AOP predictions with human AOP norms and with theoretical factor scores; analysis across training checkpoints

## Key Findings

1. LMs' predicted AOPs are closer to human preferences than predictions from any individual theoretical factor (subjectivity, frequency, syntactic class).
2. LM AOP predictions are not simply frequency matching; even for adjective pairs where both orders are rare in training data, LMs show preferences that correlate with human data.
3. The correspondence between LM and human AOPs improves over training, suggesting the models are learning structural properties rather than memorizing local distributions.
4. No single theoretical factor explains the full pattern in the LM's representations, suggesting LMs may implicitly integrate multiple cues.

## Core Takeaways For The Current Project

- Provides an independent, large-scale validation that adjective ordering preferences are learnable from language data and reflect systematic structure beyond corpus frequency — motivating the claim that ordering preferences are cognitively real and not purely epiphenomenal.
- The finding that LMs outperform any single theoretical factor suggests the true mechanism may be multi-factorial, consistent with a model like ours that combines subjectivity (via RSA utility) with contextual discriminability.
- The paper introduces a reusable corpus resource (adjective pair norms) that could potentially be used to evaluate our model's predictions against a broader set of adjective types.

## Limitations / Boundary Conditions

- The paper establishes a correlation between LM and human preferences but does not identify the mechanistic source of the LM's preferences — interpretability remains open.
- The LMs studied are autoregressive text models, not models of communicative interaction; their "preferences" are based on next-word probability, which conflates ordering preferences with collocational frequency.
- The comparison to theoretical factors relies on existing norm datasets that may themselves be limited in coverage.
