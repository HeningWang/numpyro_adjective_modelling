## One-Paragraph Summary

Scontras provides a comprehensive review of over a century of research on adjective ordering preferences, from Sweet (1898) through modern computational and cross-linguistic work. The review surveys predictors (semantic class hierarchies, subjectivity, absoluteness, inherentness, information-theoretic measures, collocation frequency), empirical methodologies (corpus, behavioral, neurolinguistic), and explanatory frameworks (grammatical/syntactic cartography, psychological/cognitive, information-theoretic/efficiency-based). It evaluates the relative success of different accounts and identifies subjectivity as currently the most empirically supported predictor, while noting that no single factor explains all the data. The review closes by identifying open questions including the source of ordering preferences in children, the relationship between preferences and grammatical constraints, and the role of context in modulating preferences.

## Research Question

## Key Findings

1. Adjective ordering preferences are robustly cross-linguistic and are found in both prenominal and postnominal languages (with the latter showing mirror-image preferences).
2. Subjectivity (Scontras et al. 2017) is currently the most successful single predictor, supported by behavioral data, corpus analysis, and computational simulation.
3. Information-theoretic measures (mutual information, integration cost, information locality) show promise but interact with subjectivity and have not supplanted it.
4. Grammatical/cartographic accounts (Cinque, Scott) provide descriptive coverage but lack explanatory power for why the hierarchy takes the form it does.
5. Context modulates preferences (Danks & Schwenk 1972; Fukumura 2018; Schlotterbeck & Wang 2023), showing that ordering is not rigidly fixed.

## Core Takeaways For The Current Project

- This review provides the canonical literature backdrop for our paper — it is the primary source for situating our model within the broader adjective ordering literature.
- Scontras explicitly discusses the efficiency-based explanations (Section 4+) and their connection to the subjectivity account; our RSA model instantiates one such efficiency explanation.
- The review emphasizes that context modulation is an important open question — our project directly addresses this by embedding adjective ordering in a referential task with manipulated discriminability.

## Limitations / Boundary Conditions

- As a review, it does not present new data or models.
- The review is necessarily selective; not all languages and adjective classes receive equal coverage.
- Written before some recent work (e.g., our own incremental RSA model's full empirical evaluation).
