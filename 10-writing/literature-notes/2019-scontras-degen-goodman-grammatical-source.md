## One-Paragraph Summary

Scontras, Degen, and Goodman follow up their 2017 paper by asking not merely whether subjectivity predicts ordering preferences but why it does. They survey and critically evaluate candidate explanations — cartographic syntax, accessibility/frequency, perception-based (Bever 1970), and functional/determination-based (Seiler 1978) — before proposing a new account tying subjectivity to the hierarchical structure of nominal modification and pressures from successful reference resolution. The key argument is that adjectives closer to the noun compose with a more restricted set of potential referents; less subjective adjectives (with more determinate, inter-speaker-consistent meanings) are more useful at this late, restricted stage of hierarchical composition, leading to their preferred placement near the noun. This account handles both prenominal and postnominal languages without positing different mechanisms.

## Research Question

- Why does subjectivity determine adjective ordering preferences? What is the grammatical and cognitive mechanism underlying the empirical effect established in Scontras et al. (2017)?

## Method / Data

- Design: Theoretical / conceptual analysis with reference to behavioral and corpus data from Scontras et al. (2017)
- Participants/Data: No new empirical data collected; relies on the database from Scontras et al. (2017)

- Analysis approach: Critical evaluation of existing accounts; formal proposal linking subjectivity to hierarchical nominal structure and reference resolution

## Key Findings

1. Cartographic accounts (Cinque, Scott) are rejected because they predict categorical (not graded) preferences and do not explain why subjectivity should determine syntactic projection hierarchy.
2. Accessibility/frequency accounts are rejected because adjective frequency applies pressure in the opposite direction (more frequent adjectives occur farther from the noun in English).
3. Bever's perceptual (nounlike) account lacks empirical support (Wulff 2003 corpus data).
4. The proposed functional account links subjectivity to reference resolution via hierarchical nominal composition: adjectives closer to the noun compose with a more restricted set, where less subjective adjectives (with more deterministic meanings) are more useful for narrow-context identification; this works symmetrically for pre- and postnominal languages.

## Core Takeaways For The Current Project

- Provides the formal grammatical motivation for why our RSA model predicts the subjectivity ordering: in the sequential intersective composition that our incremental speaker uses, the adjective placed closest to the noun restricts the smallest context, and therefore benefits most from being less subjective / more deterministic.
- The reference resolution account here is directly isomorphic to the RSA listener's posterior probability calculation: the listener incrementally narrows the referential set, and a less subjective adjective at the inner position contributes more reliable information.
- The symmetry argument for pre/postnominal languages is important for our cross-linguistic framing: the mechanism works the same way in both cases, just with different linear ordering consequences.

## Limitations / Boundary Conditions

- The functional account is argued theoretically and supported by reference to Scontras et al. (2017) data, but no new experiment directly tests the claim that less subjective adjectives are more useful in narrow reference contexts.
- The account treats subjectivity as a semantic property of adjectives without fully formalizing the link to RSA-style utilities; the formal link is developed more explicitly in Franke et al. (2019) and Hahn et al. (2018).

## Useful Quotes (Short)

- "Adjectives that are linearly closer to the modified noun are often structurally closer, composing with the noun before adjectives that are farther away. Pressures from successful reference resolution dictate that less subjective, more useful adjectives contribute their meaning to the resulting nominal earlier." (Abstract)
- "First you form the concepts... then you modify those concepts with further adjectives." (Concept formation point)

## Relevance To Current Writing Tasks

- Supports: The reference-resolution motivation for our model; the incremental composition mechanism; the cross-linguistic symmetry.
- Contrasts with: Cartographic/syntactic accounts; accessibility-frequency accounts.
- Open questions for us: Our model does not distinguish hierarchical nominal structure from linear sequence; for pre-nominal English, these coincide, but our theoretical framing should acknowledge the distinction.
