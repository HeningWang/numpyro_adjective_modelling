# Incrementality and Efficiency Shape Pragmatics Across Languages

## Metadata

- Authors: Paula Rubio-Fernandez, Julian Jara-Ettinger
- Year: 2020
- Venue: PNAS, 117(24), 13399–13404
- DOI / URL: 10.1073/pnas.1922067117

## One-Paragraph Summary

This PNAS study uses eye-tracking across four typologically unrelated languages (Catalan, Hindi, Hungarian, Wolof) to show that pragmatic reasoning during reference resolution is guided universally by incrementality and efficiency, but in language-specific ways determined by adjective-noun word order. Speakers of adjective-noun languages (Hindi, Hungarian) can derive a contrastive inference early, anticipating the noun from the adjective alone; speakers of noun-adjective languages (Catalan, Wolof) cannot and must wait for the noun. The key finding is that the same pragmatic reasoning mechanism operates universally, but word order creates different *pragmatic affordances*, resulting in different real-time interpretation patterns across languages.

## Research Question

- Do incrementality and efficiency shape pragmatic reasoning universally across languages, and does word order modulate which pragmatic affordances are available?

## Method / Data

- Design: Eye-tracking study with three types of visual displays (zero-competitor, one-competitor, two-competitor) and three adjective types (color, material, scalar)
- Participants: Native speakers of Catalan (Spain), Hindi (India), Hungarian (Hungary), Wolof (The Gambia)
- Measures: Fixation proportions to target, property competitor, kind competitor, and distractors over time
- Analysis approach: Mixed-effects models on fixation data; cross-linguistic comparison

## Key Findings

1. Speakers of adjective-noun languages (Hindi, Hungarian) resolve reference earlier in two-competitor displays by inferring contrastive use of the adjective — consistent with incremental processing.
2. Speakers of noun-adjective languages (Catalan, Wolof) do not show this early advantage; they experience temporary ambiguity that is resolved only at the noun.
3. Both language groups show comparable performance in the zero-competitor baseline, confirming that the word-order effect is specific to conditions requiring contrastive inference.

## Core Takeaways For The Current Project

- Establishes that **adjective position determines pragmatic affordance**: prenominal adjectives enable contrastive inference before the noun; postnominal adjectives do not. This is the cross-linguistic grounding for the incremental RSA speaker.
- The incremental speaker's advantage is specifically in *two-competitor* (genuine contrast) contexts, not in zero-competitor (redundant) contexts — important for calibrating where the model differences matter.
- "Pragmatic reasoning is universal, but word order creates different pragmatic affordances" — a strong framing for our paper's motivation.

## Limitations / Boundary Conditions

- Eye-tracking comprehension study only; no production data in this paper.
- Sparse displays used by design — effects may differ in denser, more naturalistic scenes.
- Wolof has no written tradition; recruitment and standardization more challenging.
