\section*{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =============================================================================

Speakers of diverse languages exhibit robust ordering preferences of adjectives (\eg, preferring ``the big brown bag'' over ``the brown big bag'').
One way to approach this regularity is through communicative efficiency: helping interlocutors coordinate quickly and reliably.
Yet ``efficiency'' can be formalized from two perspectives with opposing predictions: a \emph{hierarchical, comprehension-oriented} view in which modification composes outward from the noun (placing efficient adjectives closer to it), and a \emph{linear, production-oriented} view in which speakers benefit from placing efficient adjectives early (putting them further from the noun).
The present project pursues a unified perspective: adjective ordering is explained by \emph{incremental pragmatic reasoning}, where a speaker produces one adjective at a time from left to right and the contextual informativeness of each adjective interacts with its inherent semantic imprecision through right-to-left hierarchical structure and compositional semantics.

The project has three main building blocks:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
  \item \textbf{Model.} An incremental variant of the Rational Speech Act (RSA) model, implemented in NumPyro / JAX, with Bayesian model comparisons.
  \item \textbf{Experiments.} Two complementary experiments in referential communication---a slider-rating experiment, and a free-production experiment.
  \item \textbf{Simulation.} A broad parameter sweep showing that the size-first advantage emerges without being assumed a priori, generalising our findings beyond the limited set of experiment items.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{discussbox}{High-level research questions}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item What is the minimal pragmatic mechanism that predicts adjective ordering as an emergent consequence of communication, rather than stipulating a fixed order?
  \item What role does the interaction between \emph{inherent} semantic imprecision and \emph{contextual} informativeness play in shaping ordering preferences?
  \item Does incremental (left-to-right) production planning add explanatory power beyond what compositional semantics alone provides?
\end{itemize}
\end{discussbox}

\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Representational primitives}
\label{sec:model:primitives}

\textbf{State space.} An \textbf{object} is a triple $o = (s, c, f)$ where $s \in [1,10]$ is size (used to construct experiment items), $c \in \{0,1\}$ indicates colour match, and $f \in \{0,1\}$ indicates form match.
A \textbf{context} $\mathcal{C}$ is a set of $n_\text{obj}$ objects; the referent is always the largest object with $c=f=1$.

\textbf{Utterance space.} Utterances are built from two semantic types: \adj{S} (size) and \adj{C} (colour/form; form is subsumed under colour because they share the same determinate semantics).
An utterance $u = (a_1, \ldots, a_T)$ is an ordered sequence of adjectives $a_t \in \{\adj{S}, \adj{C}, \adj{F}\}$, where $T \in \{1,2,3\}$ is the utterance length.
Combining lengths and orderings yields 15 surface forms:
\{S, SC, SCF, SF, SFC, C, CS, CSF, CF, CFS, F, FS, FSC, FC, FCS\}.
In all analyses, \adj{F} is treated identically to \adj{C}.

\subsection{Meaning functions}
\label{sec:model:semantics}

\paragraph{Context-dependent, threshold-based size (\adj{S}) semantics.}  We adopt a \emph{slack semantics} based on the Approximate Number System.
A context-dependent threshold is computed as
\begin{equation}
  \theta_k(\mathcal{C})
  = x^\text{mid}_\text{max}(\mathcal{C})
    - k \,\bigl(x^\text{mid}_\text{max}(\mathcal{C}) - x^\text{mid}_\text{min}(\mathcal{C})\bigr),
  \label{eq:threshold}
\end{equation}
where $x^\text{mid}_\text{min}$ and $x^\text{mid}_\text{max}$ are the $q_\text{low}$- and $q_\text{high}$-quantiles of the contextual size distribution ($q_\text{low}=0.2$, $q_\text{high}=0.8$).
The semantic value of applying \adj{S} to object $o$ is then
\begin{equation}
  \llbracket\text{S}\rrbracket(o, \mathcal{C})
  = \Phi\!\left(
      \frac{s_o - \theta_k(\mathcal{C})}
           {w_f \sqrt{s_o^2 + \theta_k(\mathcal{C})^2}}
    \right),
  \label{eq:size_sem}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_k$ is the threshold from \cref{eq:threshold}, $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF, and $w_f \geq 0$ is a \emph{perceptual blur} parameter.

\paragraph{Colour/Form (\adj{C}, \adj{F}).}
Both colour and form have determinate denotations: an object either matches the described colour/form or it does not ($c, f \in \{0,1\}$).
Their semantic value is parameterised by a scalar $\nu \in (0,1)$, interpretable as the probability that a feature-matching object is correctly described by the adjective---capturing residual uncertainty in the mapping from perceptual feature to word.
In the slider model both \adj{C} and \adj{F} share a single $\nu$; in the production model they receive separate values ($\nu_\text{colour}$, $\nu_\text{form}$), but both are fixed rather than inferred (see \cref{sec:model:variants}).

\subsection{Incremental listener}
\label{sec:model:composition}

The incremental listener constructs utterance meanings compositionally by applying a chain-rule--based update over tokenwise semantics.
Crucially, interpretation proceeds \emph{incrementally} not only in the sense of multiplying semantic contributions, but also in the sense that each update \emph{redefines the context} relative to which subsequent tokens are evaluated.

\paragraph{Chain--rule factorisation.}
Let $u=(w_1,\ldots,w_T)$ be an utterance and let $o$ range over objects.
The listener maintains a sequence of belief states $\{P_t(o)\}_{t=0}^T$, where $P_t(o)$ represents the listener's posterior belief after incorporating the suffix $(w_{t+1},\ldots,w_T)$.
Formally,
\begin{equation}
  P_t(o) = P\bigl(o \mid w_{t+1},\ldots,w_T\bigr), \qquad t=0,\ldots,T,
  \label{eq:listener_belief}
\end{equation}
with base case
\begin{equation}
  P_T(o) = P_{\mathrm{prior}}(o).
  \label{eq:listener_base}
\end{equation}
For $t = T,T-1,\ldots,1$, beliefs are updated according to
\begin{equation}
  P_{t-1}(o) \;\propto\; P_t(o)\;\llbracket w_t \rrbracket_{P_t}(o),
  \label{eq:comp}
\end{equation}
where $\llbracket w_t \rrbracket_{P_t}(o)$ is the semantic value of token $w_t$ \emph{evaluated relative to the current context} $P_t$.
Normalisation yields
\begin{equation}
  P_{t-1}(o) = \frac{P_t(o)\,\llbracket w_t \rrbracket_{P_t}(o)}{\sum_{o'} P_t(o')\,\llbracket w_t \rrbracket_{P_t}(o')}.
  \label{eq:comp_norm}
\end{equation}
After all tokens have been incorporated, the listener arrives at the utterance-level posterior
\begin{equation}
  P_0(o) = P(o \mid u),
  \label{eq:listener_final}
\end{equation}
which corresponds to the appropriate row of the listener matrix $M_{\text{listener}}$.

\paragraph{Context recursion.}
A key property of this formulation is that the current belief state $P_t$ serves a \emph{dual role}.
First, it is multiplied with the semantic contribution of the next token.
Second---and critically---it also determines the semantics of that token itself.
Thus, the update from $P_t$ to $P_{t-1}$ is not merely multiplicative but \emph{recursive}:
\begin{equation}
  \llbracket w_t \rrbracket_{P_t} \quad\text{depends on}\quad P_t(o) \;\propto\; P_{t+1}(o)\,\llbracket w_{t+1} \rrbracket_{P_{t+1}}(o).
  \label{eq:context_recursion}
\end{equation}
As a consequence, the interpretation of each token is conditioned on the entire suffix that follows it.
Later words reshape the context in which earlier words are interpreted, rather than simply contributing independent semantic factors.

\paragraph{Ordering effects.}
Because certain lexical meanings---most notably size adjectives---depend on the current context distribution, changing the order of words alters the sequence of contexts $\{P_t\}$ and thereby the meanings computed at each step.
This breaks the commutativity that would hold under static or Boolean semantics:
\begin{equation}
  P(o \mid w_1,w_2) \;\neq\; P(o \mid w_2,w_1) \quad\text{in general.}
  \label{eq:noncommutativity}
\end{equation}
Incrementality therefore arises from two interacting sources: (\emph{i}) chain-rule composition of tokenwise semantics, and (\emph{ii}) recursive context dependence.
Together, these properties yield a genuinely incremental listener whose interpretations are sensitive to word order in a way that cannot be reduced to a global, order-insensitive update.

\subsection{Utterance prior (cost)}
\label{sec:model:cost}

We incorporate two cost signals into the utterance prior, depending on the dataset:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Slider rating --- subjectivity bias:} an additive penalty favouring size-first (S-initial) orderings, captured by a bias parameter $b$.
  \item \textbf{Production --- LM-derived cost:} $\text{cost}_\text{LM}(u) = -\beta\,\log P_\text{LM}(u)$,
    where $P_\text{LM}$ is a GPT-based prior over the 15 utterances and
    $\beta$ controls sensitivity.  This cost implicitly captures frequency and length biases: shorter, high-frequency forms (\eg\ \utter{S}) are cheaper.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Speaker models}
\label{sec:model:speakers}

\subsubsection{Global speaker}
\label{sec:model:speakers:global}

The global speaker computes a distribution over complete utterances by applying a softmax to utterance-level utilities:
\begin{equation}
  S_{\text{gb}}(u \mid o)
  = \frac{\exp\!\bigl(U(o,u)\bigr)}{\sum_{u'} \exp\!\bigl(U(o,u')\bigr)}.
  \label{eq:global_speaker}
\end{equation}
The utility of an utterance $u$ for an intended referent $o^\ast$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
  U(o^\ast,u)
  = \alpha\,\log L(o^\ast \mid u) + \beta\,\log P_{\mathrm{LM}}(u),
  \label{eq:global_utility}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the RSA rationality parameter governing sensitivity to informativeness, and $\beta \geq 0$ scales the contribution of the language-model prior, which encodes utterance frequency and length biases (higher $\beta$ favours high-probability LM forms).

\textbf{Slider variant.}
For the slider-rating models, which compare only two utterances (\utter{big blue} vs.\ \utter{blue big}), the utility takes the form
\begin{equation}
  U(o,u) = \alpha\bigl(\log L(o \mid u) - b_u\bigr),
  \label{eq:slider_utility}
\end{equation}
where $b_u$ is the subjectivity bias ($b_u = 0$ for the size-first ordering, $b_u = b$ for the reverse ordering).

For each intended object $o_j$, this yields a full distribution over utterances.
The resulting probability vector constitutes the $j$th row of the global speaker matrix $M_{\text{gsp}}$.
In the experiment, the speaker always refers to a fixed target referent $o_r$.
Under our indexing scheme, the target referent corresponds to index~$1$.
Accordingly, the model prediction entering the likelihood is
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{p} = (p_1,\dots,p_{15}) = P(u \mid o_r),
  \label{eq:global_prediction}
\end{equation}
which is given by the first row of $M_{\text{gsp}}$.

\subsubsection{Incremental RSA speaker}
\label{sec:model:speakers:incremental}

\paragraph{Chain-rule factorisation.}
For a fixed intended referent $o^\ast$, the incremental pragmatic speaker constructs utterances token by token.
Formally, utterance production obeys the chain rule:
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{inc}}(u \mid o^\ast)
  = \prod_{t=1}^{T} S_t\!\left(w_t \mid u_{<t},\, o^\ast\right),
  \qquad u_{<t} = (w_1,\ldots,w_{t-1}),
  \label{eq:inc_chain}
\end{equation}
where $S_t(\cdot)$ denotes the speaker's local decision rule at step~$t$.

\paragraph{Local RSA utility.}
At each step, the speaker evaluates possible next tokens $a$ solely in terms of their informativeness for the intended referent.
The local decision rule is
\begin{equation}
  S_t(a \mid u_{<t}, o^\ast)
  \;\propto\;
  \bigl[L(o^\ast \mid u_{<t} + a)\bigr]^{\alpha},
  \label{eq:inc_speaker}
\end{equation}
where $L(o^\ast \mid u)$ is the incremental literal listener defined over context-dependent semantics, and $\alpha$ is the RSA rationality parameter.
Thus, at the token level the incremental speaker reasons only about \emph{informativeness}: utterance cost does not enter into the stepwise utility.
Costs derived from the external language model apply only at the level of complete utterances, as described in the utterance-cost subsection.

\paragraph{Composition of utterance-level cost and incremental pragmatics.}
Combining the chain-rule factorisation with utterance-level cost yields the full production probability for an utterance $u = (w_1,\ldots,w_T)$:
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{inc}}(u \mid o^\ast)
  = P_{\mathrm{LM}}(u)^{\beta}
  \cdot \prod_{t=1}^{T}
  \frac{\bigl[L(o^\ast \mid u_{<t} + w_t)\bigr]^{\alpha}}{Z_t(u_{<t}, o^\ast)},
  \label{eq:inc_full}
\end{equation}
where $P_{\mathrm{LM}}(u)$ is the language-model probability of the complete utterance and
\begin{equation}
  Z_t(u_{<t}, o^\ast)
  = \sum_{a} \bigl[L(o^\ast \mid u_{<t} + a)\bigr]^{\alpha}
  \label{eq:inc_normaliser}
\end{equation}
is the local normalisation constant ensuring that $S_t(\cdot \mid u_{<t}, o^\ast)$ defines a proper probability distribution over possible next tokens.

This formulation makes explicit that utterance cost enters the model only once, as a global multiplicative factor, while pragmatic reasoning operates incrementally through successive evaluations of local informativeness.
As a result, ordering effects in the incremental speaker arise from sequential semantic context update rather than from token-level cost or language-model biases.

\paragraph{Intuition: why incremental and global speakers are not equivalent.}
Although both the global and incremental speakers assign probabilities over the same utterance space, they are not in general equivalent.
The key difference lies in how pragmatic reasoning is applied.
In the global speaker, pragmatic reasoning is applied once, at the level of complete utterances.
In the incremental speaker, pragmatic reasoning is applied repeatedly and locally, at each step of utterance construction, relative to an evolving semantic context.

Formally, in the global speaker, utterance utility is computed as
\begin{equation}
  U_{\mathrm{gsp}}(o^\ast,u)
  = \alpha \log L(o^\ast \mid u) + \beta\,\log P_{\mathrm{LM}}(u),
  \label{eq:global_utility_compare}
\end{equation}
and normalised once over complete utterances.
In contrast, the incremental speaker decomposes pragmatic reasoning across token positions:
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{inc}}(u \mid o^\ast)
  = P_{\mathrm{LM}}(u)^{\beta}
  \prod_{t=1}^{T}
  \frac{\bigl[L(o^\ast \mid u_{<t} + w_t)\bigr]^{\alpha}}{Z_t(u_{<t}, o^\ast)}.
  \label{eq:inc_compare}
\end{equation}
Because the listener $L(o^\ast \mid u)$ depends on context-dependent semantics, the marginal contribution of a word differs depending on when it is evaluated.
As a result, the product of locally optimal pragmatic choices generally does not coincide with the globally optimal utterance-level choice.
Thus,
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{inc}}(u \mid o^\ast) \;\neq\; S_{\mathrm{gsp}}(u \mid o^\ast) \quad\text{in general},
  \label{eq:speaker_inequality}
\end{equation}
even when both models share the same language-model cost and rationality parameter.

Intuitively, the incremental speaker optimises \emph{incremental informativeness}, while the global speaker optimises \emph{total informativeness}.
When semantics is context-dependent---as in the present model---these two criteria diverge, yielding genuine ordering effects that cannot be reduced to utterance-level cost differences.

\begin{discussbox}{Challenges and experiences in fitting the models}
\label{sec:model:challenges}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Computational efficiency.}
    Making inference tractable required systematic vectorisation of data and parameters (leveraging JAX's batch semantics), JIT compilation of the entire model graph, and precomputing all quantities that do not depend on MCMC samples---in particular, marginalising out stochastic dependencies that could otherwise be recomputed at every leapfrog step.

  \item \textbf{Slow and stuck chains; chain diagnostics.}
    NUTS chains were frequently slow or got stuck in low-probability regions, making the usual workflow of running many short diagnostic chains prohibitively expensive.
    This motivated a dedicated posterior-analysis pipeline to detect problems early: visual inspection of posterior distributions for bad shapes, number of divergences, cross-chain disagreement, high autocorrelation within chains, and strong parameter correlations.
    Defining what counts as ``successful inference'' (\eg\ by inspecting by-condition posterior predictives) helps a lot.

  \item \textbf{Slider rating data: bathtub-shaped distribution.}
    Slider ratings pile up at 0 and 1 (boundary values), violating standard continuous likelihood assumptions.
    We adopted a Zero-One-Inflated Beta (ZOIB) likelihood that explicitly models boundary probability mass via mixture components---a point mass at 0 with probability $\pi_0$, a point mass at 1 with probability $\pi_1$, and a Beta distribution for interior values with probability $1 - \pi_0 - \pi_1$.

  \item \textbf{Production data: degenerate and context-dependent distributions.}
    The free-production data exhibit highly concentrated, context-dependent categorical distributions---many utterance types receive near-zero counts in some conditions---making the full likelihood surface very steep and difficult to navigate.
    Our solution was to fix the semantic parameters ($k$, $w_f$, $\nu$) at shared values across both datasets and infer only the pragmatic parameters ($\alpha$, $\beta$) from the production data.
\end{itemize}
\end{discussbox}

\section{Experiments: Overall Design}
\label{sec:experiments}
% =============================================================================

The two experiments are complementary.  The slider-rating study elicits graded acceptability judgements for pre-specified descriptions, framed from the speaker's perspective (\ie, ``how well does this description pick out the target?''), providing continuous data on how well each description fits a given context.  The free-production study records the descriptions that speakers actually generate, yielding categorical choice data that reveal ordering preferences and the frequency of redundant modifiers.  Together, the two tasks separate semantic fit (\emph{how good is this description?}) from pragmatic choice (\emph{which description does a speaker produce?}).

Both experiments share the same $3 \times 2$ factorial design:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{0.88\textwidth}{lX}
\toprule
\textbf{Factor} & \textbf{Levels} \\
\midrule
$F_1$: \textbf{Relevant property}
  & Size-relevant, Colour-relevant, Both-relevant \\
$F_2$: \textbf{Sharpness}
  & Sharp (sizes well separated), Blurred (sizes clustered) \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{2}{p{0.85\textwidth}}{\textit{Fixed}: Referent = largest, colour-matching, form-matching object in a 6-object context} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}

The adjective pair shown on each trial varied (size--colour, size--form, or colour--form), but because form and colour share the same semantic type---determinate features parameterised by $\nu$---they are treated identically in the model.  All analyses are collapsed across adjective-pair types.
Each item encodes a context as a $6 \times 3$ matrix $(s, c, f)$ with the referent at index 0 (\cref{fig:item}).

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Slider-rating study (Experiment 1)}
\label{sec:exp:slider}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Task.}  Participants rated the \emph{felicity} of each adjective description on a continuous slider (0--100) for a given context-referent pair.
Ratings were collected for single-adjective (\utter{S}, \utter{C}, \utter{F}) and two-adjective (\utter{SC}, \utter{SF}, \utter{CS}, \etc) descriptions.

\paragraph{Dependent variable.}  Normalised slider ratings from [0, 100] to [-0.5, 0.5], mean-centred around 0, positive values indicating a preference for size-first orderings and negative values indicating a preference for colour/form-first orderings. 

\begin{discussbox}{Key findings (lmer: centred slider $\sim$ relevance $\times$ sharpness + (1$|$participant) + (1$|$item); \cref{fig:slider-empirical,fig:slider-model}).}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Relevance main effect} ($F(2, 3349) = 213.7$, $p < .001$; $\chi^2(2) = 402.6$, $p < .001$): size-first ordering preference is highest in size-relevant contexts, second highest in both-relevant contexts, and lowest in colour-relevant contexts.
  \item Even in colour-relevant contexts, mean centred ratings remain above zero; the reverse ordering (below zero) is qualitatively not preferred.
  \item \textbf{Sharpness main effect}: not significant ($F(1, 132) = 0.03$, $p = .86$).
  \item \textbf{Relevance $\times$ Sharpness interaction} ($F(2, 3349) = 5.84$, $p = .003$; $\chi^2(2) = 11.7$, $p = .003$): sharpness modulates the size-first advantage asymmetrically---it \emph{increases} the preference in size-relevant contexts (blurred: $0.30$, sharp: $0.34$) but \emph{decreases} it in both-relevant (blurred: $0.25$, sharp: $0.20$) and colour-relevant contexts (blurred: $0.06$, sharp: $0.04$).
\end{itemize}
\end{discussbox}

\subsection{Free-production study (Experiment 2)}
\label{sec:exp:production}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Task.}  Participants described the referent object to a hypothetical listener in as few words as possible, using only the provided adjectives.
Responses were manually annotated into the 15 utterance categories.

\paragraph{Dependent variable.}  Categorical annotation $y_i \in \{1, \ldots, 15\}$ for each trial $i$.

\begin{discussbox}{Key findings (glmer: binomial, (1$|$participant) + (1$|$item); \cref{fig:production-empirical,fig:production-model}).}

\textbf{(1) Size-first preference} (S-initial utterances coded as 1, all others as 0):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Relevance main effect} ($\chi^2(2) = 1859.7$, $p < .001$): size-first orderings dominate in size-relevant contexts; colour-first orderings dominate in colour-relevant contexts; both-relevant falls in between.
  \item \textbf{Sharpness main effect}: not significant ($\chi^2(1) = 0.79$, $p = .37$).
  \item \textbf{Relevance $\times$ Sharpness interaction} ($\chi^2(2) = 31.4$, $p < .001$): sharpness modulates the size-first advantage asymmetrically across relevance conditions---it reduces size-first preference in size-relevant contexts but increases it in both-relevant contexts.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{(2) Over-informative utterances} (three-adjective responses in both-relevant; multi-word responses in size-/colour-relevant coded as 1):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Relevance main effect} ($\chi^2(2) = 725.7$, $p < .001$): over-informative utterances are most frequent in size-relevant contexts, less frequent in both-relevant and colour-relevant contexts.
  \item \textbf{Sharpness main effect} ($\chi^2(1) = 10.3$, $p = .001$): over-informative utterances are more frequent in the blurred condition than in the sharp condition.
  \item \textbf{Relevance $\times$ Sharpness interaction} ($\chi^2(2) = 63.8$, $p < .001$): the sharpness effect on over-informativity is strongest in size-relevant contexts (blurred elicits far more multi-word utterances) and attenuated in both-relevant and colour-relevant contexts.
\end{itemize}
\end{discussbox}

\section{Simulation}
\label{sec:simulation}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Goals}
\label{sec:sim:goals}

The simulation has three purposes:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item Establish that the incremental speaker \emph{generates} ordering preferences, not just accommodates them, across a broad range of contexts.
  \item Map out which parameter region produces a qualitative \emph{big-before-colour} preference and compare to a \emph{colour-before-big} region.
  \item Assess robustness: does the ordering preference persist under varying context size ($n_\text{obj}$), size-distribution spread ($\sigma_\text{spread}$), and perceptual blur ($w_f$)?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Design}
\label{sec:sim:design}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{0.92\textwidth}{lXl}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Values swept} & \textbf{\# levels} \\
\midrule
Speaker model    & Incremental, Global        & 2 \\
$n_\text{obj}$   & 2, 6, 10, 14, 18, 22, 26, 30 & 8 \\
$\sigma_\text{spread}$ & 2.0, 7.75, 15.0 & 3 \\
$\nu$ (colour semantic value) & 0.90, 0.92, 0.94, 0.96, 0.98 & 5 \\
$k$ (threshold)  & 0.10, 0.30, 0.50, 0.70, 0.90 & 5 \\
$w_f$ (perceptual blur) & 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.8 & 5 \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{2}{l}{Random context samples per combination} & 1000 \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{2}{l}{\textbf{Total rows}} & \textbf{6,000,000} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}

The parameter $\sigma_\text{spread}$ controls the standard deviation of the truncated normal from which object sizes are drawn within a context.  
A small value (e.g.\ $\sigma_\text{spread}=2$) produces \emph{blurred} contexts in which objects are similar in size, making size adjectives less informative; a large value (e.g.\ $\sigma_\text{spread}=15$) yields \emph{sharp} contexts with well-separated sizes, making size adjectives highly informative.

Each simulated trial computes communicative success $p(o_\text{ref} \mid u)$ for two key orderings (\utter{big blue} $>$ \utter{blue big}) and records which ordering the speaker model assigns higher probability.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:sim:results}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{../../10-writing/figures/sim_advantage_nobj.pdf}
  \caption{Size-first advantage (\texttt{big blue} $-$ \texttt{blue big}) as a function of context size $n_\text{obj}$ and context sharpness $\sigma_\text{spread}$, faceted by speaker type.  Both speakers show a positive advantage in sharp contexts that diminishes with increasing context size.  Crucially, the incremental speaker exhibits a \emph{negative} advantage (colour-first preference) in blurred contexts with few objects, a pattern absent from the global speaker.}
  \label{fig:sim-nobj}
\end{figure}

\begin{discussbox}{Simulation findings}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Both speaker types generate a size-first ordering advantage across the majority of the parameter space, demonstrating that the emergence of ordering preferences is not exclusive to the incremental mechanism.
  \item The global speaker derives its ordering asymmetry solely from context-dependent, sequential-update compositional semantics---i.e., the way the literal listener composes meaning by updating beliefs object-by-object.  This is the \emph{minimal mechanism}: it requires no production-level incrementality, only a compositional semantics that is sensitive to contextual informativity.  This is compatible with previous listener-oriented communicative-efficiency accounts of subjectivity-based ordering preferences.  While those accounts relied on non-agent simulations, our explicit RSA model captures, extends, and confirms their core insight within a fully agent-based framework.
  \item The incremental speaker amplifies this effect and additionally produces qualitatively distinct predictions (e.g., a \emph{reversal} to colour-first preference in blurred, small-context conditions; \cref{fig:sim-nobj}), suggesting that the incremental production mechanism contributes an additional, separable source of ordering preferences beyond the minimal compositional-semantics baseline.
\end{itemize}
\end{discussbox}

\section{General Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Summary}

We presented an RSA model with two speaker variants---global and incremental---that derives adjective ordering preferences from the interaction of context-sensitive compositional semantics, communicative efficiency, and, in the incremental case, left-to-right production planning.  The model is fitted to two complementary behavioural datasets (slider ratings, Experiment~1; free production, Experiment~2) via NUTS-based Bayesian inference in NumPyro\,/\,JAX, and its qualitative predictions are tested with simulation.

\begin{discussbox}{Key findings}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Ordering preferences emerge from compositional semantics alone.}
    Even the global speaker---which has no production-level incrementality---generates a size-before-colour preference across most of the parameter space, because applying a color adjective first to the hierarchical compositional semantics narrows the comparison class and thereby boosts the informativeness of a subsequent size adjective (\cref{sec:simulation}).
  \item \textbf{Incremental production amplifies and qualitatively extends the effect.}
  \item Also \textbf{incremental hierarchical model fits the data best.}
  \item \textbf{Q: Do we need an incremental speaker model?}
\end{itemize}
\end{discussbox}
